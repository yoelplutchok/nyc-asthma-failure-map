Methodology

How we identified NYC's childhood asthma prevention failure zones

Overview

This project identifies neighborhoods where NYC's healthcare system fails to prevent childhood asthma emergencies. We define a "prevention failure zone" as a neighborhood with high emergency room visit rates AND low access to preventive specialists (pediatric pulmonologists and allergists).

The visualization uses a bivariate choropleth map—a technique that shows two variables simultaneously using a 3×3 color grid. This reveals spatial patterns that single-variable maps miss.

UHF 42 Neighborhoods

The United Hospital Fund (UHF) divides NYC into 42 neighborhoods for health planning purposes. This geography balances granularity with statistical reliability and is the standard unit for NYC health data.

Provider Selection

We identified providers who specialize in childhood respiratory conditions:

  • Pediatric Pulmonologists — Specialists in childhood lung diseases
  • Pediatric Allergists — Specialists in allergic conditions including asthma triggers
  • General Allergists — Many serve pediatric patients

Providers were queried from the NPI Registry using taxonomy descriptions. A total of 1,056 providers were identified with NYC practice addresses.

Geocoding & Assignment

Provider addresses were geocoded using the US Census Geocoder API, achieving a 97% success rate. Geocoded locations were then spatially joined to UHF neighborhoods using GIS techniques.

Bivariate Classification

Each neighborhood is classified on two dimensions:

Dimension 1: ER Visit Rate

Asthma-related emergency department visits for children ages 5-17, expressed as a rate per 10,000 children. Neighborhoods are divided into terciles:

  • Low (1): 23.2 to 87.7 visits per 10,000
  • Medium (2): 90.8 to 147.9 visits per 10,000
  • High (3): 153.7 to 355.8 visits per 10,000

Dimension 2: Specialist Access

The number of pediatric pulmonologists and allergists per 10,000 children in each neighborhood. Neighborhoods are divided into terciles:

  • High Access (1): 8.52 to 76.19 providers per 10,000
  • Medium Access (2): 2.87 to 7.26 providers per 10,000
  • Low Access (3): 0.00 to 2.77 providers per 10,000
Why Terciles?

Terciles divide data into three equal groups, which maps naturally to a 3×3 bivariate color scheme. This is more intuitive than quartiles or quintiles while still revealing meaningful variation.

Failure Zone Definition

A neighborhood is classified as a Prevention Failure Zone if it falls in the highest tercile for ER visits (3) AND the lowest tercile for specialist access (3). This corresponds to the deep blue color in the upper-right corner of the legend.

4 neighborhoods meet this criteria, containing 221,647 children (12.8% of NYC's child population).

Historical Redlining Analysis

We analyzed the relationship between historical redlining (1930s HOLC maps) and current childhood asthma outcomes. The Home Owners' Loan Corporation (HOLC) graded neighborhoods from A ("Best") to D ("Hazardous"), with D-graded areas—predominantly communities of color—systematically denied mortgage lending.

Key Findings

  • Significant correlation: Neighborhoods with higher historical redlining have higher ER visit rates today (r = 0.47, p = 0.002)
  • Failure zones disproportionately affected: Prevention failure zones average 36.4% historically redlined area, compared to 21.7% for other neighborhoods

Interpreting Redlining Correlation

This correlation does not imply that redlining directly causes asthma. Rather, redlining created patterns of disinvestment, housing deterioration, and environmental burden that persist today. These structural factors—not the maps themselves—contribute to health disparities.

Limitations

Important Caveats
  • Provider location ≠ Provider access. A specialist in a neighborhood may not accept Medicaid, have capacity for new patients, or be culturally accessible.
  • Cross-boundary care. Patients often travel to specialists outside their neighborhood. Manhattan's high concentration serves patients citywide.
  • Correlation ≠ Causation. High ER rates may reflect poverty, housing conditions, air quality, or other factors beyond specialist availability.
  • Data currency. NPI data reflects registered addresses which may lag actual practice patterns.
  • Suppressed values. Some ER rates are marked with asterisks in source data, indicating small numbers that may be unreliable.

Reproducibility

This analysis was built with full reproducibility in mind. All code, data processing scripts, and documentation are available in the project repository. The pipeline uses:

  • Python 3.11 with pandas, geopandas, shapely
  • Structured JSONL logging for full pipeline traceability
  • Atomic file writes with metadata sidecars
  • Configuration-driven parameters (no magic numbers in code)
